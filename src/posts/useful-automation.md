Most automation fails for the same reason. It solves a problem nobody was struggling with, or it solves the right problem in a way that just becomes something new to maintain.

## The question I ask first

Before I build anything, I ask who is doing this manually right now, and what it costs them. Not in theory. Literally: what do they click, copy, and re-check every day?

If I can't answer that in one sentence, I'm not ready to automate anything.

## Three traits that separate automation from clutter

- **They remove a step, not add one.** If people still have to check the automation's work as often as they'd do the task themselves, it hasn't earned its place.
- **They fail loudly and locally.** A broken flow that silently drops data is worse than no automation at all.
- **Someone other than the builder can explain what they do.** If only I understand a workflow, it's not infrastructure, it's a liability with my name on it.

> A dashboard nobody opens is not automation. It's decoration with a database behind it.

## The test that actually matters

A bot that generates reports nobody reads. A Power Automate flow duct-taped to a SharePoint list. These all look like progress on a slide.

The test I use is simpler: would the person doing the work miss it if it broke tomorrow?

If the honest answer is no, it wasn't solving a real problem in the first place. It was solving a *visibility* problem, and those are cheaper to fix with a five-minute conversation than a workflow engine.
